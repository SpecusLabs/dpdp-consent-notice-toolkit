# Section 7 — Legitimate Use (Processing Without Consent)

**A plain English breakdown of when you can process personal data without obtaining consent under the DPDP Act, 2023.**

> **Disclaimer:** This is not legal advice. Review with a qualified professional before use.

---

## What Section 7 Says

Section 7 of the DPDP Act lists specific situations where a Data Fiduciary may process personal data without consent. These are called "legitimate uses."

This section exists because requiring consent for every data use would make ordinary business and government functions impossible. You cannot, for example, ask an employee to consent to payroll processing — you have a legal obligation to pay them and deduct tax.

---

## The Legitimate Use Categories

### 7(a) — State Functions
**Who it applies to:** Central Government, State Governments, and their instrumentalities.

**What it covers:** Processing data in the interest of the sovereignty and integrity of India, security of the State, friendly relations with foreign states, maintaining public order, or preventing incitement of any cognizable offence.

**For private businesses:** This does not apply to you. Move to the next category.

---

### 7(b) — Compliance with Judgement or Order
**Who it applies to:** Any person or entity.

**What it covers:** Processing personal data in compliance with any judgment or decree of a court or tribunal in India, or an order by a quasi-judicial authority.

**Example:** You receive a court order requiring you to produce a customer's transaction records. You may process and share that data without the customer's consent.

**What to do:** Document the court order. Keep a copy on file. Do not disclose more data than the order requires.

---

### 7(c) — Medical Emergency
**Who it applies to:** Any person or entity.

**What it covers:** Processing data to respond to a medical emergency involving a threat to the life or immediate health threat to the Data Principal or another individual.

**Example:** A food delivery company shares a customer's address with emergency services when the customer reports a medical emergency through the app.

**What to do:** This is a narrow exception. It applies only to genuine medical emergencies, not to general health-related services.

---

### 7(d) — Public Health and Safety
**Who it applies to:** The State or any Data Fiduciary acting under a State direction.

**What it covers:** Processing data to respond to a public health emergency, epidemic, outbreak, or disaster or breakdown of public order.

**Example:** Government-mandated contact tracing during a pandemic.

**For private businesses:** Generally does not apply unless you are acting under an explicit government direction.

---

### 7(e) — Employment-Related Purposes
**Who it applies to:** Employers — any entity that employs or engages workers.

**What it covers:** Processing data for the purpose of employment or safeguarding the employer from loss or liability, including:
- Hiring and onboarding
- Payroll processing
- Performance management
- Attendance tracking
- Background verification (within legal limits)
- Statutory compliance (PF, ESIC, TDS, gratuity)

**Important caveats:**
- The processing must be reasonably expected in the context of employment
- You cannot use employment as a basis to process data for unrelated purposes (e.g., selling employee data to third parties)
- The Data Principal (employee or worker) should still be informed of what is collected and why — even if consent is not required, notice is still good practice

**Example for labour/staffing companies:**
Collecting a worker's Aadhaar, PAN, and bank account details for PF registration, TDS deduction, and salary payment falls under Section 7(e). You do not need separate consent for this. However, you must still tell the worker what you're collecting and why — and you must not use this data for any other purpose without consent.

---

### 7(f) — Performance of Functions of State or Subsidies, Benefits, and Services
**Who it applies to:** Entities processing data to provide subsidies, benefits, services, certificates, licences, or permits authorised by law.

**Example:** A government portal processing Aadhaar to verify eligibility for a subsidy scheme.

**For private businesses:** Applies only if you are providing a government-authorised service or benefit.

---

## What Legitimate Use Does NOT Mean

Section 7 is a defined set of exceptions. It is not a general permission to process data without consent whenever it is convenient.

**Common misunderstandings:**

| Claim | Correct? |
|-------|---------|
| "We don't need consent because it's for legitimate business purposes" | ❌ No — Section 7 lists specific purposes only |
| "We don't need consent from employees for anything" | ❌ No — only employment-related processing |
| "We can use employee data for marketing because Section 7 covers employment" | ❌ No — marketing is not an employment purpose |
| "We collect PAN for KYC so Section 7 applies" | ✅ Likely yes if required by law (e.g., PMLA) |
| "We pay salaries so Section 7 applies to payroll data" | ✅ Yes |

---

## Documenting Your Legitimate Use Basis

If you rely on Section 7 instead of consent for a particular data use, document it. Keep a record that says:

> "We process [data type] for [purpose] under Section 7([subsection]) of the DPDP Act, 2023 because [reason]."

**Example:**

> "We process worker Aadhaar numbers and bank account details for the purpose of PF registration and salary disbursement under Section 7(e) of the DPDP Act, 2023, in our capacity as employer."

This documentation protects you if the Data Protection Board ever questions your processing.

## Notice Is Still Good Practice

Even when Section 7 applies and consent is not required, issuing a notice to the Data Principal is still strongly recommended. It:

- Builds trust with workers and customers
- Reduces grievance filings
- Demonstrates good faith to the Data Protection Board
- Prepares you for future regulatory tightening

Use the notice templates in `02-consent-notice-templates/` even when you are relying on Section 7.
